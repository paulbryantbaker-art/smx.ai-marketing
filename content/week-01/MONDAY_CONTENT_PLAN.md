## POST 1: SELLERS — Small Business Network (314K)

### Post Copy

Two HVAC companies. Same city. Same revenue. Same year.

One sold for 7.2× EBITDA. The other got 3.1×.

On $4M EBITDA, that's $28.8M vs $12.4M. The gap: $16.4M.

Not different businesses. Different preparation.

The 3.1× owner showed up with tax returns and a handshake. Two customers made up 40% of his revenue. He ran every bid personally. Three PE firms looked. All three passed. He took a lowball from a strategic because he was tired.

The 7.2× owner spent 18 months getting ready.

Dropped concentration from 35% to 12%. Built a management team — VP Ops, sales director, service manager. Stopped attending customer meetings 9 months before listing. Ran a sell-side QofE: $1.2M in documented adjustments. Every number had a backup binder.

The PE firms that passed on the first one fought over the second.

Same industry. Same EBITDA. Same market. $16.4M apart.

The gap between 3× and 7× isn't luck. It's not timing. It's not finding a better broker. It's 18 months of specific work that most owners don't know exists.

What would a buyer flag first if they looked at your business tomorrow?

## POST 2: BUYERS — Startup Specialists (986K)

### Post Copy

How a PE investor compares 20 businesses and picks the winner.

4 numbers. 90 seconds. 15 are dead before anyone reads a single page.

This isn't theory. This is the exact screen a $500M fund applies before their analyst spends one minute on a deal. And it works the same whether the business is $500K or $50M.

Number 1: Customer concentration. Any single customer above 25% of revenue — kill. I watched a $3.2M deal die 11 days before close. The 38% customer picked up the phone and said "we're leaving." SBA lender pulled out in 48 hours. Four months of work. $60K in sunk costs. Dead.

Number 2: SDE margin. Below 15% — kill. On $1M revenue with 15% margins, you've got $150K in earnings. Debt service takes $60K. Owner salary takes $80K. That leaves $10K for everything going wrong. One bad month and you're underwater on a personal guarantee you can't escape.

Number 3: Revenue trajectory. Three consecutive quarters declining — kill. Sellers price on the best year. Buyers who pay peak prices for a shrinking business are buying a story, not a company.

Number 4: Owner dependency. Remove the owner on paper. Does the business still function? If every client relationship, every sale, every decision runs through one person — you're not buying a company. You're buying a job. And the person who does that job is leaving.

15 out of 20 businesses fail at least one of these. The PE associate moves on in 90 seconds. The first-time buyer spends 4 months and $50K discovering the same thing.

The difference between professional buyers and everyone else isn't intelligence. It's knowing what to check before you fall in love.

What would you add as a 5th screen?

## POST 3: EXECUTIVES — Executive Suite (380K)

### Post Copy

How a strategic seller exits at the top of the EBITDA range while everyone else fights for scraps at 3×.

The median sub-$5M business sells for 2.5-3.5× SDE. The median lower-middle-market deal ($5M-$15M EBITDA) trades at 4.5-6.5×. But inside every range there's a top quartile getting 30-50% above median — and a bottom quartile taking whatever the market offers.

The top quartile does four things. Every time.

They run a process, not a negotiation. One buyer gives you a price. Three buyers give you a market. They engage 3-5 qualified buyers simultaneously. The highest price comes from competition, not persuasion. A $20M EBITDA manufacturer turned down $100M from the one buyer who'd been circling. Ran a real process — 52 contacted, 18 NDAs, 4 finalists. Closed at $140M. Same company. $40M more. Different process.

They control the narrative. The CIM isn't a data dump — it's a strategic document. An IT services company repositioned from "managed services provider" to "recurring-revenue platform in a $200B fragmented market with 95% retention." Same business. 9.4× instead of 5.5×. $15.6M gap.

They fix problems before listing. Concentration, dependency, financial presentation — every issue a buyer would flag is addressed before the first meeting. Due diligence confirms value. It doesn't discover risk.

They know their number cold. Not a range from a broker. Data-backed. Normalized earnings, comparable transactions, and a strategic premium argument ready to anchor the conversation. They set the frame. Not the buyer.

The difference between 3× and 7× isn't the quality of the business. It's whether the seller ran a strategic process or just "put it on the market."

What's the one thing most sellers underestimate about the exit process?

## POST 4: DEAL PROS — SearchFunder (9.4K)

### Post Copy

A search fund operator closed 3 deals in 14 months. Everyone else in his cohort closed zero.

The difference wasn't deal flow. Wasn't connections. Wasn't a better thesis. It was what he did in the first 90 seconds with every deal.

He screened 1,200 businesses. Not 3,000 — because he killed 60% of them in under 2 minutes based on four quantitative thresholds. Concentration above 25%: next. Margins below 18%: next. Revenue declining two quarters: next. Owner IS the operation with no transition plan: next.

He never ran QofE on a deal that failed the screen. Total busted deal costs over 14 months: $12K. His cohort averaged $85K.

He signed 5 LOIs. 3 closed. All three passed the 4-screen test on first look.

The 3 deals: environmental services ($2.1M EBITDA, 5.2×), specialty logistics ($1.8M EBITDA, 4.8×), and facilities maintenance ($2.4M EBITDA, 5.5×). All had managers in place. All had diversified revenue. All had clean financials.

His cohort screened more businesses. Attended more management presentations. Spent more money on advisors and diligence. And closed nothing. Because they spent 4 hours per deal falling in love with revenue before checking the fundamentals.

He told me: "My cohort was looking for the perfect deal. I was looking for the fastest no. Everything else is time management."

The advantage in deal sourcing isn't access. Everyone has the same brokered channels. It's velocity through the funnel — how fast you can say no to the 85% that will never close so you can spend real time on the 15% that might.

What's your fastest deal-kill? The signal that made you walk in under 2 minutes?
